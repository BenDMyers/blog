## Introduction

The north star for web accessibility is [<dfn>POUR</dfn>](https://www.w3.org/WAI/fundamentals/accessibility-principles/)â€”web content should be perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust. <dfn>Robustness</dfn> ensures that your content isn't just perceivable, operable, and understandable, but that it's all of those things regardless of the browsers, devices, and assistive technologies that users use to access your page.

How do we ensure that our pages are robustly accessible? The following are five things that run through my head when I evaluate the solutions I or my teammates have written:

As developers, we provide value to users by meeting their needs. We can only do that when we understand what they need in the first place.

Perceivability, operability, understandability, and robustness are [functional requirements](https://www.altexsoft.com/blog/business/functional-and-non-functional-requirements-specification-and-types/#functional-requirements-and-their-specifications) for all of your users. We usually take that for granted, however, because asserting that users should understand and be able to interact with our page feels like such an obvious thing to say. When it comes to disabled users, however, these requirements are often not well-understood or taken into account.
