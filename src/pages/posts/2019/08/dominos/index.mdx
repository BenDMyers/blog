<b>Guillermo Robles has been fighting a battle for nearly three years now.</b> It started in <time datetime="2016-09">September 2016</time>, when he brought a suit against Domino's Pizza, alleging the chain's website was inaccessible via a screenreader. Since then, the case has moved up the ladder as more and more courts have weighed in on the question of whether websites are legally required to be accessible. In 2017, the Central District of California dismissed Robles's case on the grounds that there weren't clear enough standards to hold Domino's to. In <time datetime="2019-01">January 2019</time>, the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals overturned that dismissal. According to the Ninth Circuit, the absence of clear standards didn't absolve Domino's of their legal requirement to make their website accessible. In <time datetime="2019-07">July 2019</time>, Domino's appealed to the Supreme Court to shut this case down again. They're backed by other big businesses and the National Retail Foundation.

Unfortunately, cases like Robles's are not uncommon. Disabled users cannot guarantee that as they navigate the web, they will have an accessible experience. As the world around us becomes increasingly powered by the web, ordering pizza isn't the only thing that could be out of reach.
